Introduction to CPR & AED

"Imagine this: You're walking down a street, and someone collapses right in front of you. Their breathing stops. What would you do?"

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), sudden cardiac arrest (SCA) claims over 7 million lives annually, many of which could be saved with immediate CPR.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand when and why CPR is necessary
  • Learn the steps of hands-only CPR for adults
  • Master CPR techniques for children and infants
  • Learn proper AED usage and safety protocols

Understanding Sudden Cardiac Arrest

Key Point: Every minute without CPR decreases survival chances by 7-10%.
Signs of SCA
  • Sudden collapse
  • No pulse
  • No breathing or only gasping
  • Loss of consciousness
Risk Factors
  • Heart disease
  • Previous heart attack
  • Family history
  • Advanced age

Adult CPR

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Check Scene & Response

    Ensure the scene is safe, then tap and ask loudly "Are you OK?"

  2. Call Emergency Services

    If unresponsive, call emergency services or ask someone else to call

  3. Check Breathing

    Look, listen, and feel for breathing for no more than 10 seconds

  4. Begin Compressions
    • Center of chest
    • 100-120 compressions per minute
    • 2 inches deep
    • Allow full chest recoil

Child & Infant CPR

Important: Different techniques are required for children and infants.
Child CPR (Ages 1-8)
  • Use one or two hands
  • 2 inches compression depth
  • Check breathing first
  • Give rescue breaths if trained
Infant CPR (Under 1 Year)
  • Use two fingers only
  • 1.5 inches compression depth
  • Compress just below nipple line
  • Gentler force than adult CPR

AED Usage

Remember: AEDs are designed for public use and will guide you through each step.

AED Steps

Power On

Turn on the AED and follow voice prompts

Attach Pads

Place pads on bare chest according to diagram

Clear the Area

Ensure no one is touching the person

Follow Prompts

Press shock button if advised, then resume CPR
